Playing Pong inside a diffusion model

A 1/1000-scale reproduction of MIRA (Multiplayer Interactive World Models with Representation Autoencoders): a two-player, real-time, audio-video world model of 16×16 Pong, trained on one RTX 5080 using the official mira codebase. No game engine runs at play time — every pixel and every beep is denoised from latents, conditioned on both players’ key presses.

10.9M-param DiTflow matching + diffusion forcing 2 players20 fps + 8 kHz audio ~5 h video training~70 min audio training

1 · The training data

The dataset mirrors MIRA’s Rocket Science recordings at toy scale: 400 episodes × 50 s of bot-vs-bot play (780k player-view frames). Each episode records, per frame: both players’ first-person views, each player’s multi-hot key state ([Up, Down]; neither = stay), sound-event flags, and the privileged physics state (ball position/velocity, paddle positions, score) — the latter used only for evaluation probes, never by the model.

Things to notice, because the world model has to learn all of them: the two views are mirror images of one shared world (each player sees their own paddle on the left, in blue); the score pips on the top row are a persistent HUD; the key lights are the only control signal the model ever gets; and the sound effects are time-locked to what happens on screen.

How it was generated

Following the paper’s bot-only collection (they used the Nexto policy; no human data): a scripted ball-tracking bot plays both paddles, with per-episode reaction delay (1–3 frames) and action-noise injection (half of all players get 5–20% random actions — the paper’s noise procedure) for behavioral diversity. Three background tints stand in for the three Rocket League arenas. The whole vectorized numpy simulation generates the dataset in about one second; it ships as a 24 MB npz.

2 · How it trains on mira’s code

The point of the exercise was to reuse the official codebase, not re-implement it. Everything scientifically load-bearing is imported from src/mira unmodified:

Reused verbatim

  • DiffusionTransformer — the DiT itself
  • ActionEncoder — per-key embeddings + pooling
  • flow-matching + diffusion-forcing loss
  • PSD few-step self-distillation loss
  • streaming KV-cache rollout (denoise_streaming)
  • MultiWrapperWorldModel — view tiling, per-player action fusion, SP→MP warm-start
  • ViTVideoDecoder — the codec’s causal space-time decoder
  • adaptive loss weighting, ModelEMA

Different for the toy

  • Frozen feature extractor: DINOv3-L can’t run on 16×16 images, so a 0.2M ViT is pretrained self-supervised (masked patch reconstruction) and frozen in its place
  • Training loops: their Hydra/torchrun/W&B multi-node trainers are replaced by ~100-line single-GPU loops with the same published hyperparameters (AdamW 1e-4, β=(0.9,0.99), wd 0.1, 1k-warmup→constant, grad clip 1.0, EMA)
  • Two ~30-line subclasses swap which codec class loads (their VideoCodec hard-requires DINOv3 weights)
  • 2 players instead of 4, one shared screen rendered as 2 mirrored views

Training follows the paper’s exact staging: single-player pretrain → multiplayer warm-start → few-step distillation. The warm start used mira’s built-in remapping and transferred every parameter; multiplayer loss started at 0.34 instead of the ~1.9 cold-start value.

multiplayer = the two views stacked along height into one latent grid (8×4), so spatial attention spans both players’ views in a single pass — that is where cross-view consistency comes from.

At play time the loop is exactly the paper’s streaming design: hold a rolling window of 20 latents, append one noise latent, denoise it in 2 flow-matching steps conditioned on the freshest key presses through a streaming KV-cache, decode 2 video frames + 100 ms of audio, drop the oldest latent, repeat. One step takes ~11 ms compiled — ~9× faster than the 100 ms real-time budget (the 5B paper model needs ~70 ms of a B200 for the same loop).

4 · Adding sound: the pointwise-sum trick

The paper’s codec is video-only, but its sibling systems at Kyutai handle audio with a neural codec (Mimi) and fuse streams by adding embeddings pointwise per timestep. The toy does the same thing inside mira’s unchanged transformer:

  • Sound effects are classic-Pong square waves synthesized from the env’s event flags: a quiet 90 Hz movement tick, a 226 Hz wall bounce, a 459 Hz paddle hit, a 490 Hz score jingle (8 kHz mono, 400 samples per video frame).
  • A tiny audio codec (a conv autoencoder, the Mimi analog) compresses each 100 ms chunk — exactly one latent frame of time — into 8 channels.
  • That per-frame audio latent is broadcast over the 4×4 spatial grid and concatenated to the video latent channels. Because the DiT embeds tokens with a single linear layer, a linear over concatenated channels is mathematically the sum of two linear projections — i.e. a learned audio embedding pointwise-summed into every spatial token of that frame, the same additive fusion the architecture already uses for actions and flow time.
  • Flow matching + diffusion forcing then denoise the 16-channel latent jointly: there is no separate audio model, no event detector at inference — the same denoising step that decides the ball bounced also decides the air vibrates.
# the whole trick, in encode_video():
z_video = codec.encode(frames)                     # (b, t, 4, 4, 8)
z_audio = audio_codec.encode(chunks)               # (b, t, 8)
z_audio = z_audio[:, :, None, None, :].expand(b, t, 4, 4, 8)
z = torch.cat([z_video, z_audio], dim=-1)          # (b, t, 4, 4, 16) -> unchanged DiT

Generating audio at inference: the denoised latent’s last 8 channels are averaged over the spatial grid (they were broadcast at encode time, so this is a small ensemble over the denoised copies), un-normalized, and pushed through the audio codec’s decoder — 100 ms of waveform per latent step, queued to the speaker alongside the 2 decoded frames.

Because only the DiT’s input/output projections changed width (8→16 channels), the trained video-only model warm-started the audio model: 324 of 329 parameter tensors transferred, and 25k steps sufficed. Measured audio-video binding in the model’s own imagination: when its generated video shows a wall bounce, its generated audio contains the wall beep within ±2 frames 84% of the time (paddle hits: 70%).
